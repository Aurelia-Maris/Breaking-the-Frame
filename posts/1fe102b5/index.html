EU Enlargement Is More Geopolitical - But Not Less Bureaucratic
Serbia’s Commission reports show that Brussels has added geopolitical urgency to enlargement monitoring without abandoning its old fundamentals-based script.


The ritual begins again.

In 2025, the European Commission once more published its annual report on Serbia. The chapter headings were familiar. So were the concerns: judicial independence, corruption, media freedom, democratic institutions, civil society, Kosovo normalisation, and foreign policy alignment. For six consecutive reporting cycles since 2020, Brussels has restated much of the same reform script. Serbia, meanwhile, has made no meaningful progress on the fundamentals.

This is the paradox at the heart of EU enlargement monitoring. The EU keeps writing. Serbia keeps not changing. And the exercise repeats.

By 2025, the annual Serbia report looks less like a reform instrument with credible consequences than a bureaucratic ritual: a document that preserves the appearance of conditionality, absorbs geopolitical pressure, and keeps the accession process formally alive without changing the underlying script.

Two patterns across six reporting cycles make the case.

The European Commission’s Serbia reports between 2020 and 2025 show a striking pattern of thematic continuity. Across six consecutive reporting cycles, thirteen of fifteen coded recommendation categories appear without interruption. Judicial independence, corruption control, media freedom, democratic institutions, and civil society space are listed, assessed, and found wanting - year after year. The language sharpens slightly in the later reports, particularly after 2023, but the architecture of demands does not change. What the Commission asked of Serbia in 2020, it was still asking in 2025.


This is not what a functioning reform instrument looks like. Repeated recommendations without structural change in what is recommended suggests that the monitoring framework has become self-referential: it documents the same deficits it documented before, restates the same expectations, and moves on to the following year. The reports remain technically accurate. But accuracy and consequence are not the same thing.


The geopolitical record tells a similar story.


After Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022, the geopolitical register of the Serbia reports changed markedly. References to foreign policy alignment, sanctions-related language, and Kosovo normalisation became substantially more frequent in the 2023, 2024, and 2025 cycles. The Commission’s tone acquired a new urgency. Enlargement was reframed as a geostrategic investment, and Serbia’s position - maintaining accession candidacy while declining to align with EU sanctions on Russia - received sharper attention.


Yet the underlying reform script did not change. Geopolitical pressure was absorbed into the existing monitoring framework rather than restructuring it. The fundamentals chapters remained in place, their demands largely intact. What shifted was emphasis, not architecture. This matters because it reveals something about how the monitoring system handles external pressure: it accommodates new political urgencies by adding weight to certain issues, without revisiting what the absence of consequences for non-compliance means. The reports became more geopolitically attentive. They did not become more credible.

The cost of ritualised monitoring is not only that reform remains slow. It is that stagnation becomes administratively manageable. Serbian authorities can continue to point to an active accession process as evidence of engagement with Europe, while the domestic costs of deeper reform and fuller foreign-policy alignment remain politically avoidable. Brussels, in turn, can point to the annual reports as evidence that conditionality remains intact, while leaving ambiguous what repeated non-compliance should trigger in practice. Other candidate governments may draw the same lesson: the reform script is demanding, but it can also be waited out.


This is why ritualised monitoring matters. It does not simply describe stagnation. It helps make stagnation governable.


The alternative is not abandoning Serbia or replacing conditionality with geopolitical bargaining. It is making clear what repeated non-compliance changes in practice, not in the next report’s language, but in the actual terms of the accession relationship. If the same fundamentals are listed year after year, but the political consequences remain ambiguous, monitoring becomes a way of managing stagnation rather than overcoming it.


The real test will come with the next report. If the 2026 document looks almost identical to the 2020 one, the question will not be whether Brussels has forgotten how to write conditionality. It will be whether conditionality still has consequences.
