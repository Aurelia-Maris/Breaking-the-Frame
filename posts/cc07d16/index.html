In EU Enlargement, the Normal Track Is Now the Problem
A.Maris
  2026-05-06   2026-05-06  
  • Policy Watch
  • Western Balkans
  • EU
  876 Words  5 Mins  

Ukraine and Moldova are often treated as the special cases in today’s EU enlargement debate. But the more revealing problem may lie in the “normal” track: a Western Balkans accession process where conditionality remains formally intact, while political arrival keeps receding.

Ukraine is the exception. The Western Balkans are the warning.

In June 2024, the EU formally opened accession negotiations with Ukraine and Moldova. In the same cycle, the Commission continued to assess the Western Balkans through its familiar annual progress reports. Both were presented under the same enlargement framework. But they were not the same exercise.

The Western Balkans have been inside the enlargement process for decades. Serbia applied in 2009. Montenegro in 2008. North Macedonia has held candidate status since 2005. Yet none of them are close to membership. Ukraine and Moldova applied in spring 2022 and opened formal negotiations just over two years later.

The EU’s dual-track problem is not simply that Ukraine and Moldova moved faster. It is that their acceleration exposes what has happened to the normal track: conditionality still produces reports and recommendations, but no clear sense of political arrival. The most revealing evidence is not only Ukraine’s speed, but Serbia’s repetition.

Serbia and the repetition of conditionality

Across the European Commission’s Serbia reports from 2020 to 2025, the core reform script remains highly stable: rule of law, judiciary, corruption, media freedom, democratic institutions, civil society, Kosovo normalisation and foreign policy alignment recur across reporting cycles, while progress remains limited or uneven. In my analysis of these reports, thirteen of fifteen coded recommendation categories appear in every single reporting cycle. Much of what the Commission asked of Serbia in 2020 was still being asked, in recognisably similar form, in 2025. That repetition matters because conditionality derives its credibility not only from the clarity of its demands, but from the belief that meeting them can alter the political trajectory.

Repeated recommendations without substantial change in what is recommended suggest that monitoring risks becoming self-referential: it documents the same deficits, restates the same expectations, and moves on to the following year. Accuracy and consequence are not the same thing. Conditionality remains formally intact, but it increasingly resembles a long-term governance arrangement rather than a route to a defined endpoint.

When geopolitics changes the accession clock

Ukraine applied on 28 February 2022, days after Russia’s invasion, and received candidate status on 23 June 2022. Moldova followed an almost identical trajectory. Both opened accession negotiations on 25 June 2024, roughly two years and four months after applying.

The Western Balkans experienced no comparable compression. Serbia took four years from application to talks. Albania waited over thirteen years. North Macedonia applied in 2004 and did not open formal negotiations until 2022. These timelines are not directly comparable in every political or institutional respect. The Western Balkans have their own unresolved disputes, domestic reform deficits and member-state blockages. But the contrast still matters: accession time is not governed by reform assessment alone.

The contrast is not merely an external interpretation. In December 2025, Commission President von der Leyen described enlargement as “a strategic choice” and made the temporal logic explicit: “the speed of the accession process needs to match the pace of geopolitical shifts.” Ukraine and Moldova do not demonstrate that reforms no longer matter. They demonstrate something more precise: reform performance is not the only clock governing accession politics.

The credibility problem is in the normal track

The deeper problem is that the EU now appears to be operating two accession logics under the same enlargement vocabulary. For Ukraine and Moldova, membership is not only a question of institutional compliance, but of security commitment and strategic anchoring. For the Western Balkans, accession remains tied to a slower conditionality script: assessed, criticised, advised and reassessed, with the political reward appearing increasingly distant.

This creates a credibility problem. If the Union tells Western Balkan candidates that reforms are the route to progress, while also saying that accession speed must match geopolitical shifts, it needs to explain how these two logics coexist. Otherwise, candidate governments have reason to recalculate the practical value of compliance. It is not because rules are irrelevant, but because geopolitics has visibly altered the clock. This is a structural observation about incentives, not a moral argument about fairness.

Can the normal track still promise arrival?

The question is whether the EU can maintain a coherent account of what enlargement means when it is operating two different versions of it simultaneously.

The EU does not need to apologise for treating Ukraine and Moldova as strategically urgent cases. But it does need to explain how strategic acceleration can coexist with a merit-based process for the Western Balkans. Without that explanation, the normal track risks looking less like a demanding route to membership than a holding pattern.

If the 2026 Western Balkans reports look almost identical to those of 2020, the issue will not be whether Brussels has forgotten how to write conditionality. It will be whether conditionality still looks like a credible road to membership, or whether it has become a language for managing candidates whose arrival remains indefinitely postponed.

 

This article draws on the author’s independent analysis of European Commission Serbia Reports from 2020 to 2025.
